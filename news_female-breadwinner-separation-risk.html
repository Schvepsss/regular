She Earns More and You Have Kids: A 29-Country Study Names the Real Risk Factor

By Elizaveta Shvets · July 13, 2026

Couples where women outearn men are 36% more likely to separate — and that number jumps to 49% when they have children together. A new study of 544,911 couples across 29 countries tracked this pattern for 16 years. The surprise finding: it's not gender attitudes driving it. It's who absorbs the domestic labor when kids are in the picture.

Led by Pilar Gonalons-Pons at the University of Pennsylvania, the team tested five competing explanations for why female-breadwinner couples break up more often. Traditional gender norms? Not the main driver — countries with more conservative gender attitudes didn't show a stronger pattern. Women's financial independence making it easier to leave? Didn't account for it either. What did: work-family conflict. Specifically, the gap in who manages the invisible load at home when a high-earning woman doesn't scale back from work after having a child. The 49% figure only appeared in couples with kids; childless female-breadwinner couples were 23% more likely to separate — elevated, but nowhere near the same level.

The data came from large international household surveys covering 29 high-income countries between 2004 and 2020. The finding is not a statement about female breadwinners being a problem — it's a finding about what actually creates friction. The tension lives not in who earns what, but in who carries what, daily, when both people are depleted.

What it means for you: If your partner earns more and you're both running on empty since the baby arrived, the strain you're feeling isn't about money or ego — it's about the unspoken negotiation over the invisible work. Naming that explicitly tends to be step one.
