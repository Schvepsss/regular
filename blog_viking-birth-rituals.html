Fatherhood, through time

Viking Birth Rituals: How the Norse Welcomed a Newborn

By Elizaveta Shvets · Editor-in-Chief, Regular · June 2026 · 6 min read · sources below

★

Vikings welcomed a newborn with a tight set of rituals: they sprinkled the baby with water and spoke its name (ausa vatni), gave a name-gift (nafnfestr), and had the father publicly accept the child by setting it on his knee. Birth itself was guarded with runes, songs, and appeals to goddesses like Frigg and Freyja. Most of this survives in sagas and law codes recorded in the 12th-13th centuries.

A thousand years before parenting books, the Norse had a remarkably structured way of bringing a child into the world — part legal procedure, part magic, and, at the center of it, a father publicly claiming his kid. Here's what they actually did, what the sources say, and why one detail still feels strikingly modern.

One caveat up front: almost everything we know comes from texts written down generations after the pagan era, so historians read the details carefully. That's exactly why points where several independent sources agree are the ones worth trusting.

The water-and-name rite (ausa vatni)

The central rite was ausa vatni — "to sprinkle with water." The child was lifted, water was poured over it, and its name was spoken aloud. It predates Christianity, so it isn't a borrowed baptism, and it did something powerful: it turned a newborn into a legal person who could inherit, be sheltered under the family roof, and receive the protection of the clan.

The legal weight of the moment is the striking part. Before the sprinkling and naming, the infant had almost no social existence. After it, the child was fully inside the kin-group — and, under the later law codes, killing a named child counted as murder, while the period before the rite did not carry the same status. Water and a name, in other words, switched a human being "on" socially.

The ninth night, and the father on the high seat

The rite was often held on the ninth night after birth, once it seemed more certain the baby would survive. The child was brought to the father seated on the household's high seat; he took it onto his knee, water was sprinkled, the name was spoken, and the infant was admitted to the family. Norse law held that every child must have a father, and it was that paternal recognition that made the child "his."

The timing is quietly humane. With high infant mortality, the community waited before performing the rite that made the newborn a full person. A child the father declined to accept fell into a precarious status — literally "un-carried" — with serious legal consequences. The whole sequence put the father's public acknowledgment at the emotional and legal heart of the welcome.

The name-gift (nafnfestr) and naming for luck

Naming came with a gift — the nafnfestr, literally "name-fastening." The father or name-giver handed the newborn something of value: a ring, a weapon, sometimes land. And names were chosen carefully, often after a recently deceased relative, because the Norse believed a name carried that person's luck-force (hamingja) to the child.

This makes a Viking name less a label than an inheritance. A dying elder might specifically ask that a coming child be named after him, passing on his luck and keeping his name alive among the living. The gift sealed it: a tangible token marking the child's place in the line. Identity, fortune, and property were fastened together in a single ceremony.

Birth-runes and the goddesses of labour

The most magical rite protected the birth itself. The Eddic poem Sigrdrífumál describes bjargrúnar — "helping-runes" or birth-runes — written on the palms and across the joints of the birth helper, who called on the dísir (protective female spirits) for aid. Families also appealed to goddesses tied to motherhood, fertility, and healing: Frigg, Freyja, and Eir.

Birth-runes learn, if help thou wilt lend, / the babe from the mother to bring; / on thy palms shalt write them, and round thy joints, / and ask the fates [dísir] to aid.Sigrdrífumál, st. 9 (Poetic Edda, trans. H. A. Bellows, 1923)

Births happened at home in the longhouse, attended by female relatives, neighbours, and experienced midwives who prepared herbs and charms. Researcher Katherine Olley of the University of Cambridge notes that Old Norse literature frames birth as a communal, threshold moment of "becoming" — and that the danger spikes precisely when a labour is prolonged, eased only when help arrives in time. Runes on the hands, goddesses overhead, and skilled women at the bedside were all part of one safety system.

The dark side — and why it's often overstated

Infant exposure (barnaútburðr) did exist in the pagan period, tied mainly to severe deformity or hardship and hinging on the father's acceptance. But it was an exception, not a routine ritual, and later law codes abolished it. Modern scholarship is cautious: a 2026 peer-reviewed critique argues that earlier claims of widespread female infanticide were exaggerated.

When Iceland adopted Christianity around the year 1000, exposure was briefly kept as "old law," but by the time the Grágás laws were written in the 12th century the practice was being abolished. Historian Maren Clara von Mallinckrodt (2026) shows that sweeping claims of habitual girl-killing rest on selective readings and methodological flaws. The honest summary: it happened, but rarely, and far less than older popular accounts suggested.

The detail that still feels modern

Strip away the runes and the legal machinery and one thing stands out: the Norse built the father's public acceptance into the very center of welcoming a child. A thousand years ago, the culture encoded a simple idea — a baby's arrival is about the father's involvement, too, not only the mother's.

That instinct survived the Viking Age. New dads today still want to feel central to their child's arrival rather than sidelined by it — and that pull toward involvement, toward being claimed into the new family, is something Regular is built around. The rituals changed; the underlying need didn't.

Frequently asked questions

What rituals did Vikings do when a baby was born?

Vikings welcomed a newborn with a cluster of rites: sprinkling the baby with water and naming it (ausa vatni), giving a name-gift (nafnfestr), and the father publicly accepting the child by taking it onto his knee. Birth itself was protected with charms, galdr songs, and appeals to goddesses such as Frigg, Freyja, and Eir. These customs are recorded in the sagas and law codes written down in the 12th-13th centuries.

What was the Viking water ceremony for babies (ausa vatni)?

Ausa vatni, meaning "to sprinkle with water," was the Norse naming rite: the child was lifted, water was poured over it, and its name was spoken aloud. It predates Christianity and is not a borrowed baptism. Crucially, it conferred social and legal personhood — only after being sprinkled and named could a child inherit, be sheltered, and receive the clan's protection.

Why did Vikings name babies on the ninth night?

The naming often took place on the ninth night after birth, once it was clearer the infant was likely to survive. Infant mortality was high, so the community delayed the formal rite that turned a newborn into a full member of the family. Before the rite the child had no legal standing; after it, killing the child counted as murder.

Which gods did Vikings pray to during childbirth?

During pregnancy and labour, Norse families appealed to goddesses linked with motherhood, fertility, and healing — chiefly Frigg, Freyja, and Eir — with offerings and galdr songs. Helpers also invoked the dísir, protective female spirits. Sources describe birth as a communal effort backed by both human midwives and these unseen protectors.

What were Viking birth-runes (bjargrúnar)?

Bjargrúnar, or "helping-runes," were birth-runes described in the Eddic poem Sigrdrífumál. They were written on the palms and across the joints of the person assisting the birth, while the helper called on the dísir for aid. They represent the magical side of Norse birth: runes plus invocation to bring mother and child safely through labour.

Did Vikings really practice infant exposure?

Infant exposure (barnaútburðr) existed in the pagan period, tied mainly to severe deformity or hardship, and hinged on the father's acceptance. But it was an exception, not a routine ritual, and later law codes abolished it. Recent scholarship — including a 2026 peer-reviewed critique by Maren Clara von Mallinckrodt — argues that earlier claims of widespread female infanticide were exaggerated and methodologically flawed.
