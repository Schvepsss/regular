Relationship science

A good relationship and a steady income both protect your health. Neither can do the other's job.

By Elizaveta Shvets · July 2, 2026

A new cross-national study of 1,973 adults in the US and Spain found that having a happy, responsive romantic relationship independently predicts better mental and physical health — even after accounting for income and education. But here's the catch: money stress has its own separate impact, and love can't offset it.

The research, led by Hannah Williamson at the University of Texas at Austin and published in the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, compared how relationship quality and financial status independently shaped health outcomes across two countries with very different social safety nets.

The clearest finding: feeling genuinely heard and cared for by your partner was one of the strongest predictors of both mental and physical health — stronger than actual income. But a good relationship doesn't substitute for economic stability, and economic stability doesn't substitute for a good relationship. Both matter. Independently. They don't trade off.

One detail worth noting: subjective sense of your financial standing — how you feel about your position, not just the number — predicted health more consistently than actual income. Which means: constant financial worry does its own damage regardless of what's actually in the account. Something a lot of new parents know intuitively.

What it means for you: If you're grinding under money stress and relationship friction simultaneously after a baby — both are real stressors pulling in different directions. Fixing the connection between you two won't make the financial anxiety disappear, but it does give you separate protection that money can't buy.

Source: Valor-Segura, Alonso-Ferres & Williamson, Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, June 27, 2026 · via PsyPost
