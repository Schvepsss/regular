Why Conflict Hits Harder After Baby — And What a 2025 Study Says About the Hidden Buffer

A 2025 study in the Journal of Marital and Family Therapy tracked 196 first-time parent couples across 14 daily diary entries at 4 months postpartum. The finding: it's not how much couples fight that predicts relationship damage — it's what's underneath the fight. Couples with higher underlying intimacy and lower attachment anxiety showed significantly less erosion even after the same conflicts.

By Ivan Yamshchikov · Research Editor · July 2026

The study — what they actually did

Researchers at the Université de Sherbrooke recruited 196 first-time parent couples and had them fill out daily diaries for 14 consecutive days at the four-month postpartum mark. Each day, participants reported whether they had experienced conflict with their partner and how satisfied they felt with the relationship.

The design matters. Rather than asking couples to recall how things had been "generally," the daily diary method captures what's actually happening — conflict on a Tuesday, mood on a Wednesday, the real texture of life with a four-month-old. That's much harder to distort than a retrospective self-report.

Then the researchers looked at who it hit hardest.

The finding — it's not the fight, it's the floor underneath it

Daily conflict predicted lower relationship satisfaction across the board — that part was expected. But the magnitude of the damage varied enormously by two factors: romantic attachment (specifically anxiety and avoidance) and emotional intimacy. Couples who had built a stronger baseline of closeness showed measurably less erosion after the same fights.

Think of it as load-bearing capacity. The same 200kg lands very differently on a solid floor than on a floor that's already cracked. The conflict is the same weight. The floor is what determines whether anything breaks.

Couples with high attachment anxiety — a heightened sensitivity to perceived rejection or abandonment — were hit hardest by conflict. The researchers noted that the already-hyperactivated attachment system characteristic of anxious attachment becomes further destabilized by the constant novelty and uncertainty of new parenthood. When conflict arrives in that context, it carries extra charge.

"Parents with high attachment anxiety are more likely to feel overwhelmed by the uncertainty and novelty of caring for a first child — and with an already hyperactivated attachment system, they may engage in conflict with more confrontational strategies." Lessard et al. (2025) · Journal of Marital and Family Therapy

Conversely, couples who reported higher emotional intimacy showed a buffering effect: their daily satisfaction didn't nosedive as steeply after conflict. The closeness acted as a reserve — something to draw on when the argument was over and the silence settled in.

Why four months is the flashpoint

The study focused on four months postpartum — not by accident. This is the window in which the initial adrenaline of newborn survival has worn off but the couple hasn't yet adjusted to their new normal. The baby is no longer brand new. Neither is the exhaustion. Everyone's reserves are running low.

Research by Shapiro, Gottman, and Carrere (2000) established that 67% of couples experience a clinically significant drop in relationship satisfaction in the first three years after a first child. The Lessard study zooms in on the mechanism operating at the worst moment of that decline.

What this means tonight

You cannot avoid conflict in the first year. What you can do is build the floor — the daily deposit of emotional closeness that cushions any given fight. This is not a metaphor. It is the mechanism the research describes.

The practical implication is counterintuitive. Most people think about managing conflict: how to fight better, how to de-escalate, how not to say the thing they'll regret. All of that matters. But the Lessard study suggests the more upstream intervention is building intimacy on the days when there's no fight at all.

John Gottman's research has long identified the 5:1 ratio — five positive interactions for every negative one as the threshold of relationship health. The Lessard findings offer an explanation of why: those five small moments aren't just nice to have. They are the structural material out of which the buffer is made.

What counts as a positive moment at four months postpartum? Not a date night. Not a deep conversation about feelings. A specific thanks. Noticing something out loud. Two minutes of actual eye contact before someone's phone lights up. These aren't grand gestures. They're the floor you're laying.

The argument on a Thursday lands differently when Wednesday included three of those moments.

The argument on a Thursday that follows a week of silence — logistics only, baby talk, the machinery of survival — lands on nothing. And the research shows what that costs.

FAQ

Why do couples fight more after having a baby?

New parenthood dramatically increases stressors — sleep deprivation, role shifts, less couple time, and competing demands — all of which raise the frequency and intensity of conflict. A 2025 study in the Journal of Marital and Family Therapy (Lessard et al., 196 couples, 14-day diary at 4-month postpartum) confirmed that daily conflict reliably predicts lower relationship satisfaction in new parent couples. The conflict itself is normal; the question is what's cushioning it.

Does fighting mean our relationship is in trouble after the baby?

Not necessarily. The Lessard et al. (2025) study found that the same level of conflict had very different effects on relationship satisfaction depending on the couple's underlying attachment security and intimacy. Couples with higher intimacy and more secure attachment showed less damage from daily conflict. The fight isn't always the problem — the absence of a cushion underneath it is.

What is attachment anxiety in relationships after a baby?

Attachment anxiety refers to a heightened fear of abandonment or rejection by your partner — a sensitivity that can be amplified during the first year of parenthood when roles shift radically and emotional bandwidth is low. The Lessard 2025 study found that parents with higher attachment anxiety experienced conflict as more damaging to their relationship satisfaction than those with lower anxiety. Small consistent reassurances matter more than grand gestures.

How do I reduce conflict with my wife after the baby?

The research suggests the most effective approach is not eliminating conflict (impossible in the first year) but building the underlying buffer: daily micro-moments of connection that establish a baseline sense of "we're in this together." Gottman's research shows a 5:1 ratio of positive to negative interactions predicts relationship health. Five small moments of connection — a look, a word, a specific thanks — for every difficult exchange.

What does intimacy mean for new parents?

In the context of new parenthood research, intimacy is not primarily sexual. It refers to emotional closeness — the sense of being known, seen, and connected to your partner as a person rather than as co-managers. The Lessard et al. (2025) study found that this form of intimacy acted as a buffer between daily conflict and relationship satisfaction: couples who felt emotionally close handled the same arguments with significantly less damage.

Can the relationship damage from fighting after a baby be reversed?

Yes. Research on the transition to parenthood consistently shows that deliberate, consistent small actions — not one big repair conversation — are what rebuild relationship health. A 2025 systematic review of relationship app studies (15 studies) found that structured daily digital check-ins produced meaningful improvements in relationship satisfaction. The recovery isn't one conversation. It's a hundred small ones.
