Relationship Statistics After a Baby: What Research Says

Research across 50+ studies shows: about 67% of couples experience a measurable drop in relationship satisfaction in the first three years after a baby (Shapiro, Gottman & Carrere, 2000). The sharpest decline hits in year one — confirmed by a 2022 meta-analysis of 145,139 people. And it rarely resolves on its own: data from an 8-year longitudinal study shows the gap persists. This page collects every verified statistic, with primary sources.

By Elizaveta Shvets · Editor-in-Chief, Regular · July 2026

67% — the number that keeps showing up

I first encountered this figure reading Gottman's work late one night while building Regular. I assumed it might be an outlier — one study's finding, maybe overstated.

Then it appeared again. And again. In different countries, different decades, different methodologies. A 2022 meta-analysis of 49 studies found the same thing in 145,139 people. It's not an outlier. It's what happens.

67%

of couples experience a measurable decline in relationship satisfaction within the first three years after their first child's birth.

Shapiro, Gottman & Carrere, 2000 · Journal of Family Psychology · PubMed 10740682

The 33% who don't decline tend to share one thing: they began preparing — emotionally and practically — before the baby arrived. After the fact, there's no going back to before. But there is forward.

The first year: where most of the damage happens

The decline isn't gradual. It's steep, and it front-loads.

The largest meta-analysis on the subject — 49 studies, 145,139 participants — found that the sharpest drop in marital satisfaction occurs in the first 12 months after birth, for both partners. Not just the mother. Both.

145,139

people across 49 studies — the most comprehensive meta-analysis on relationship satisfaction in the transition to parenthood. Both partners show significant decline in year one.

Bogdan, Turliuc & Candel, 2022 · Frontiers in Psychology · PMC9350520

Knowing this doesn't make the first year easier. But it does mean that what you're feeling in those sleepless months isn't evidence that something is wrong with your relationship. It's evidence that you're in year one.

Does it get better? Eight years of data

The honest answer: not automatically.

Doss, Rhoades, Stanley and Markman followed 218 couples from before marriage through eight years of observations. Couples who had children showed an abrupt and sustained decline in relationship quality — one that persisted throughout the entire study period without active intervention.

8 years

The "abrupt decline" in relationship quality after a first baby persisted across the full 8-year observation window — it did not self-correct.

Doss, Rhoades, Stanley & Markman, 2009 · Journal of Personality and Social Psychology · PubMed 19254107

This isn't to say recovery is impossible — it's to say it doesn't happen by waiting. "It'll get better eventually" is not what the research says. "It requires something active" is.

Fathers: the data that gets skipped

Most relationship research after a baby focuses on the couple or the mother. But fathers show up in the data too — and what they show is worth knowing.

Postpartum depression in fathers is real. A JAMA meta-analysis of 43 studies, covering 28,004 fathers, found that 10.4% of new dads develop postpartum depression — a figure that rises to 24–50% when the mother also has PPD.

10.4%

of new fathers develop postpartum depression — rising to 24–50% when the mother also has PPD. Peak risk period: 3–6 months after birth.

Paulson & Bazemore, 2010 · JAMA, 303(19) · JAMA

The peak arrives later than you'd expect. A separate meta-analysis of 47 studies (20,728 fathers) found that paternal depression peaks at 3–6 months postpartum — not immediately after birth, but when the exhaustion compounds and the novelty fades.

Rao et al., 2020 · Journal of Affective Disorders · PubMed 31757623

"Present but invisible" is a documented experience, not a metaphor. A 2021 peer-reviewed study documented fathers across perinatal settings consistently describing themselves as the third wheel — included physically, excluded emotionally — in a system built around the mother-infant dyad.

"While I did go to all the midwife appointments, and while I did go to all the scans, it very much felt like I was sat there as the third wheel." Father participant — Rominov et al., 2021 · BMC Pregnancy & Childbirth · PMC7913323

Her side of the data

The same period is physiologically demanding for mothers in ways the statistics rarely capture cleanly. But one figure keeps appearing.

~80%

of new mothers report sexual or intimate difficulties in the postnatal period — a figure the Gottman Institute cites across their synthesis of postnatal research.

Gottman Institute · gottman.com

When both partners are struggling — him feeling invisible and her touched out — the distance isn't about love. It's about two people in the same crisis, each unable to see the other's clearly.

What these numbers don't tell you

Statistics are population-level patterns. Yours isn't a data point — it's a relationship.

What the research does tell you: the distance you're feeling is not a personal failing. Not a sign your relationship was weak to begin with. Not evidence that something is permanently broken.

It tells you that the default path is downward — and that staying there is what happens when nothing changes, not what has to happen. The couples who come through this period tend to have something specific in common: they stopped waiting for it to feel easier on its own, and started doing one small thing instead.

Not a big conversation. Not a therapy programme. One small, repeated thing.

That's harder to quantify. But it's also what the data points toward.

FAQ

What percentage of couples have relationship problems after a baby?

About 67% of couples experience a measurable drop in relationship satisfaction in the first three years after the birth of their first child, according to Shapiro, Gottman & Carrere (2000), Journal of Family Psychology (PubMed: 10740682). This finding has been replicated in over 50 independent studies.

How long does relationship decline last after having a baby?

Research by Doss, Rhoades, Stanley & Markman (2009), tracking 218 couples for 8 years, found that the quality decline after a first baby persisted throughout the entire follow-up period. The sharpest drop is in the first 12 months, but recovery is not automatic — couples who don't actively work on their relationship tend to stay at the lower baseline.

Do new dads get postpartum depression?

Yes. A meta-analysis of 43 studies covering 28,004 fathers (Paulson & Bazemore, 2010, JAMA) found that 10.4% of new fathers develop postpartum depression. That risk jumps to 24–50% if the mother also has postpartum depression. The peak risk period for fathers is 3–6 months after birth.

Is it normal to feel like roommates with your wife after having a baby?

Yes, and the research explains why. The demands of new parenthood leave couples with almost no time for couple-specific connection. A 2022 meta-analysis of 145,139 people (Bogdan, Turliuc & Candel, Frontiers in Psychology) confirmed that both partners experience this shift in the first year.

Why do new fathers feel invisible after having a baby?

A 2021 peer-reviewed study (Rominov et al., BMC Pregnancy & Childbirth) found that fathers across perinatal care settings consistently described feeling 'present but invisible' — sidelined by systems and dynamics that prioritize the mother-infant dyad. At home, the same pattern often continues: fathers feel like the third wheel in their own family.

Does having a baby always hurt a relationship?

Not automatically — but the research is clear that the default trajectory is downward. The 33% of couples who maintain or improve satisfaction after a baby tend to share specific protective factors: emotional communication before the baby arrives, shared sense of meaning, and an intentional transition. Decline is the norm; stability requires active effort.
