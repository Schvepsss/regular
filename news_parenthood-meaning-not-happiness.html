Dad life

Having a baby won't make you permanently happier. Research says it does something better.

By Elizaveta Shvets · July 2, 2026

A global study of 5,556 parents across 10 countries finds that having children doesn't raise baseline daily happiness, sadness levels, or life satisfaction — at all. But it does produce a measurable increase in something different: a sense of meaning in life. The joy of parenthood comes in spikes, not as a new normal.

The research, led by Menelaos Apostolou at the University of Nicosia and published in Evolutionary Psychology, analyzed parents and non-parents from China, Greece, Japan, Peru, Poland, Russia, Spain, Turkey, the UK, and Ukraine. After controlling for relationship status — which turns out to matter far more than kids for day-to-day happiness — parenthood's effect on mood essentially disappeared. What remained was a modest but consistent increase in purpose and meaning.

The researchers call it the "neutrality paradox": evolutionarily, you'd expect raising children to feel great. Instead, emotional responses to kids appear to be motivational spikes rather than permanent upgrades — a brief surge of joy when your baby laughs, not a raised baseline. Your brain isn't broken for feeling exhausted between those moments. That's by design.

One finding new dads rarely hear: the study found that having children was slightly associated with lower relationship satisfaction. Not catastrophically — but the financial pressure, time demands, and identity shift do leave a mark on the couple.

As Apostolou put it: "Having people in your life whom you love unconditionally and who love you back unconditionally is a major positive life outcome, and it is the primary reward of parenthood. That reward is simply not something our study was designed to capture."

What it means for you: If you're not feeling happier since the baby arrived, you're not doing it wrong — you're doing it exactly as it was designed. The meaning is real. The baseline just doesn't shift the way anyone told you it would.

Source: Apostolou et al., Evolutionary Psychology, June 16, 2026 · via PsyPost
